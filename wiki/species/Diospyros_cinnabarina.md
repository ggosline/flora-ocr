# *Diospyros cinnabarina* "(Gurke) F. White"

**Family**: [[Ebenaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Diospyros]]

* This species is clearly very close to *D. simulans* (no. 32). The observed differences, involving calyx, corolla, and style pubescence, leaf size and degree of glabrescence, venation pattern, and gland distribution, seem in the end too slight to justify specific rank. They suggest that *D. simulans* should be treated as a synonym of *D. cinnabarina*, though field study and geographic analysis may later support infraspecific distinctions.

P1. 4, 1-10, p. 51, et CARTE 29, p. 170.

Shrub reaching 15 m in height; bark black, apparently smooth but with superficial, anastomosing vertical fissures; bark section with a black outer rhytidome band, then a red fibrous layer; sapwood white, grading into reddish-brown heartwood. Young branchlets olive, grayish, blackish, or black, covered with a fine pubescence that is quickly shed.

Leaves with petiole up to 8 mm long; blade more or less ovate, elliptic, or often lanceolate, up to 14 x 6 cm (18 x 8 cm in *D. winkleri*), with cuneate or sometimes somewhat rounded base and an acute acumen at the apex; midrib slightly impressed above; 3-5(-6) pairs of lateral veins running toward the margin; tertiary veins and veinlets practically invisible; blade papery, dull reddish above and pinkish-gray or violet beneath on dried specimens, but actually bluish glaucous green when fresh; the lower surface bears short appressed hairs, denser beneath the veins; small black glands scattered beneath the blade, at least in its lower half and especially near, though not directly on, the midrib.

Male inflorescences with 3-9 flowers, perhaps 15-25 in the original diagnosis and on old twigs, or 30-40 in *D. winkleri* on old wood, fascicled in the axils of leaves or leaf scars and accompanied by tangled, minute, pubescent deltate bracteoles. Male flowers with pubescent pedicel about 2 mm long, 8 mm in *D. winkleri*; calyx campanulate, 3 mm long, sparsely covered outside with short stout hairs and densely hairy inside, with 3-4 triangular lobes reaching about two-thirds of the calyx depth; corolla narrowly conical in bud, narrowly bottle-shaped at anthesis, then 12 mm long and 2.5 mm wide, with 3 lobes making up about one quarter of the corolla height, glabrous except on the lobes, which are sparsely covered with short stout hairs; 8-10 unequal stamens inserted on the receptacle, with filaments 0.5-2 mm long, pubescent at the base, and glabrous anthers 1.5-3.5 mm long with a distinct apicule about 1 mm long; apparently no pistillode.

Female inflorescences with 5-25 flowers, subfascicled in the axils of leaf scars or on older branchlets. Female flowers with stout pedicels 2.5-5(-10) mm long; calyx campanulate, 4.5 mm long, scarcely accrescent later, with broadly ovate-triangular lobes and otherwise like that of the male flowers; corolla conical in bud, then 8 x 3 mm, with 3 lanceolate lobes representing one third of the corolla height, coriaceous and pink; 6-7 lanceolate staminodes attached at the base of the corolla tube, about 5 mm long, glabrous; ovary ovoid-conical, 4.5 x 3 mm, densely tomentellous with short stout hairs, 6-locular; styles united into a short column ending in 3 deltate, erect-recurved, glabrous stigmas.

Fruits with stout pedicel up to 1 cm long, broadly ovoid-conical, 3.5-4 x 3-3.5 cm, orange-red, covered with fine verrucae and puberulent at least toward the apex, like the ovary; 6 black seeds, 16 x 6 mm, or 24 mm long according to the diagnosis.

**Lectotype designated by F. White (1956):** Zenker 3466, Cameroon (BM; iso-BR, E, G, K, L, MO, W).

This species is chiefly known from Cameroon and is localized in the coastal evergreen humid forest zone. It may also have been collected by Mildbraed (no. 5132, not 5312, cf. F. White 1963, lapsus) in Congolese evergreen forest between Assobam (present-day Mpan) and Lomie, although that collection (HBG) might also belong to *D. simulans* F. White. F. White also suggested that the species occurs in southwestern Nigeria; its presence is certain in southeastern Nigeria (Talbot s.n., Oban), in Gabon where it appears to be rare in the Fernan Vaz region at the mouth of the Ogooue, and in the Republic of the Congo in the Sibiti region (Farron 4401).
