**Family**: [[Ebenaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Diospyros]]

Specimens of this species appear in various herbaria under the following name: *Diospyros lecomtei* (Pierre ms.).

Tree to 25 m tall, with a rather short cylindrical bole (to about 15 m) but
sometimes very large, 80-120 cm in diameter, slightly fluted at the base with
some ascending column-like ridges and crowned by a few rather erect major
branches. Bark blackish-grey to greenish-grey, finely longitudinally striate,
peeling in fairly thick scales. In oblique section the bark shows a brittle
black outer rhytidome and, inside, a layer first pale salmon pink with cream
bands and then yellowish with orange vertical streaks; the cambium is uniformly
orange. Sapwood very thick, cream to reddish-yellow, sometimes with black
bands. Heartwood black and very hard, developing only in trees at least about
30 cm in diameter; old trees may also become hollow. Older twigs grey to
reddish-brown with shallow longitudinal cracks; young twigs slender, drying
black, glabrous. Buds silvery-pubescent with appressed hairs. Young leaves
reddish violet, later pale green.

Leaves with a dark-brown channelled petiole up to 15 mm long, glabrous or with
only a few hairs visible at high magnification. Blade lanceolate,
elliptic-lanceolate, or oblong-lanceolate, 10-21 × 5-9 cm, broadly cuneate at
the base and very slightly decurrent onto the top of the petiole; often pinched
in dried material because of a curved midrib and slight asymmetry of the blade,
with a similar constriction near the apex. Apex abruptly acuminate, with a very
narrow acumen, often slightly twisted, usually 12-18 mm long and blunt-tipped.
Blade subcoriaceous, its margin very narrowly rolled under in dried specimens,
both surfaces brownish-green when dry; upper surface glossy and glabrous, lower
surface on young leaves with a few very short, thick, appressed hairs,
especially near the base of the midrib. Small black round glands occur near the
margin toward the base of the blade, at the junctions of veins and especially
of minor veins. Midrib impressed above and very prominent beneath; 5-8 pairs of
lateral veins slightly raised below, strongly ascending and slightly curved,
linked by tertiary veins that are more or less perpendicular to the midrib.

Inflorescences axillary or, especially in female flowers, below the leaves on
older twigs. Male flowers in groups of 3-6; female flowers solitary or in
pairs, more rarely more. All are subsessile, with a few imbricate, broadly
triangular bracts and bracteoles at the base, blunt at the apex, pubescent
outside and ciliate on the margin. Calyx and corolla broadly similar in male
and female flowers. Calyx pinkish red, strongly coriaceous, hemispherical, about
10 mm long, sparsely puberulent outside and pubescent within with short dense
appressed hairs, divided into 4-5 broad triangular lobes with blunt tips not
reaching halfway down the calyx. Corolla densely short-puberulent, white at the
base and pink above, fleshy, ellipsoid and tapered at both ends, surmounted by
4-6 small suborbicular lobes 2-4 mm long; total length 25-30 mm, diameter about
15 mm at the middle.

Male flowers with very numerous white stamens, 40-110, inserted on the
receptacle in at least two circles; the bases of the filaments of adjacent
stamens from concentric cycles are often radially fused. Filaments very short,
2-3 mm; anthers linear, 10-12 mm long, acute, slightly pubescent dorsally with
hairs directed upward. Pistillode conical, pubescent, 2-3 mm tall.

Female flowers with staminodes fewer than the stamens of male flowers, inserted
at the base of the corolla in two circles and more or less fused in radial
pairs; they are generally more fusiform than the anthers, shorter
(6-8 mm), and more densely clothed with appressed hairs directed upward. The
ovary is massive and ribbed, about 5 mm high, usually with (6?-)8-10 locules,
covered with an erect velvety golden puberulence, and topped by (3-)4-5 styles
fused at the base, about 6 mm long, spatulate, their inner face erect,
expanded, and stigmatic, becoming leaf-like; the styles are also finely
puberulent.

Fruits more or less ellipsoid, up to 10 × 6.5 cm, yellow at maturity, sparsely
puberulent with short appressed hairs near the apex, surrounded at the base by
the somewhat enlarged calyx, spread like a saucer and up to 4 cm across. Flesh
creamy white to yellowish, with 4-8 brown, glossy, oblong, slightly reniform
seeds up to 4.5 × 1.5 × 1 cm.

**Type:** Thomson, Nigeria.

Known in Cameroon as "Mevini" and in Gabon as "Evila", this is the principal
source of African ebony. The wood is marketed as short logs of heartwood,
usually not exceeding 30 cm in diameter. The species occurs in Nigeria,
Cameroon, Central African Republic, both Congos, and Gabon. The decline in
ebony trade in Cameroon and Gabon may reflect past overexploitation around
Yabassi, Edea, and Libreville. More importantly, the treatment suggests that
*D. crassiflora* is not truly a species of the wettest evergreen rain forest;
instead it is more typical of old-forest patches, even semi-deciduous ones,
farther inland, or of evergreen enclaves inside semi-deciduous forest with
Sterculiaceae and Ulmaceae. Thus, although known near Victoria and Kribi, it
also occurs around Mamfe, Lomie, Bafia, Yaounde, Abong Mbang, Bertoua, and
Yokadouma. Likewise, it seems absent or at least rather rare around Libreville,
again avoiding the wettest forest.
