# *Tetracera alnifolia* Willd. subsp. *alnifolia*

**Family**: [[Dilleniaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Tetracera]] · **Section**: *Tetracera*

Sp. pl. ed. 4, **2(2)**: 1243 (1799).

## Synonymy

- *Tetracera dinklagei* Gilg, Bot. Jahrb. Syst. **33**: 201 (1902).
- *Tetracera djalonica* A.Chev. ex Hutch. & Dalziel, Fl. W. trop. Afr. **1**: 155 (1927).

Only the type subspecies occurs in Gabon. Subsp. *dinklagei* (Gilg) Kubitzki —
treated as a synonym of *T. alnifolia* by Hawthorne & Jongkind (2006) — differs
in its rough and sometimes pubescent follicles and is known only from Liberia.

## Description

Liana reaching at least 6 m in height; stem up to 10 cm in diameter; bark
greenish brown, the rhytidome detaching in plates or strips; slash greenish
brown; wood pale yellow; branchlets with reddish brown rhytidome, smooth,
glabrous or with very sparse appressed hairs.

**Leaves**: petiole **winged**, 0.5–2 cm, glabrous or with short appressed
hairs; blade elliptic to ovate or obovate, (5–)8–20(–27) × (3–)4–11(–14) cm,
acute to rounded at base — usually abruptly decurrent on the petiole — rounded
to very shortly acuminate at apex, the margin generally entire (sometimes
undulate or dentate in the upper portion), coriaceous, smooth or slightly
scabrid beneath, glabrous except for sparse appressed hairs on the underside
of the nerves (sometimes also at the base and margins when young); shining
dark green above, pale to yellowish green beneath. Lateral nerves in 7–16
pairs, curved parallel to the margin or more rarely reaching it; tertiary
venation scalariform and very dense.

**Inflorescence**: terminal, **many-flowered**, a panicle of 3–5-flowered
cymes, reaching 25 cm long and conspicuously exceeding the subtending leaf;
axes sparsely pubescent to tomentellous or glabrescent; peduncle 2–5 cm;
bracts narrowly elliptic to ovate, up to 7 × 2.5 mm, often quickly caducous.

**Flower**: pedicel 2.5–7(–10) mm, glabrous or pubescent to tomentellous; bud
4–6 mm in diameter; sepals **5(6), ± erect, remaining appressed against the
carpels in fruit**, broadly elliptic to ovate, 5–8 × (2.5–)3.5–6 mm, slightly
cucullate, gradually larger from outside to inside, ciliate, glabrous to
shortly pubescent externally on the parts exposed in bud, glabrous within;
pale green or greenish white at flowering, often becoming reddish in fruit.
Petals 2 or 3, obovate, 5–7 × 3–4 mm, rounded and entire at apex, white.
Stamens very numerous; filament glabrous, 3–8 mm; anther yellow. Carpels
3(4), glabrous; style white.

**Fruit**: with 1–3(4) ± oblique to spreading follicles, ellipsoid to ovoid,
**7–10 × 5–7 mm**, acuminate or with a beak 1–4 mm long; light brown to
reddish brown, red, or purple-brown, shining, smooth, glabrous.

**Seed**: ovoid to subglobose, 3–5 mm long, black; aril exceeding the seed.

## Distribution

**Range**: widespread in West Africa (Senegal to Nigeria), Cameroon, Príncipe,
Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, Republic of the Congo, the extreme west of the
Democratic Republic of the Congo (Mayombe), Angola and Zambia.
**Gabon**: principally in the coastal region (Estuaire, Ogooué-Maritime,
Nyanga), spreading inland through the Ogooué basin to the lower Ngounié
(Moyen-Ogooué).

## Habitat and ecology

Dense forests on terra firma or flooded ground, gallery forests, riverine
forests, secondary forests, forest edges (often behind mangroves or coastal
savannas), generally on sandy soils; in Gabon at 5–120 m altitude, elsewhere
up to 1000 m.

## Vernacular names and uses

**French**: *liane à eau*, *liane du voyageur* — "water liana", "traveller's
liana." **Nzébi**: *léghaghéla*. **Fang**: *nzine-ndzic*. **Mpongwé**: *oléni*.
**Benga**: *uléni*.

This liana is sought after by local people and foresters, who extract a clear,
drinkable water from the stems and use it for cooking. It calms colic. Nursing
women drink the same water after macerating slices of sweet cassava in it.
Pregnant women consume the powder of dried stems cooked with peanuts. A
decoction of the leaves is sometimes used against dysentery.

## Notes

- This species is delimited here in a narrower sense than previously: see
  the note on [[Tetracera_podotricha]], which was long treated as a synonym
  of *T. alnifolia* but is now distinguished without ambiguity (especially in
  Gabon). *T. alnifolia* in this sense is mainly coastal in the Gulf of Guinea,
  while *T. podotricha* is more widely distributed in central Africa.
- In the Gulf of Guinea islands, only *T. podotricha* occurs on São Tomé, and
  only *T. alnifolia* on Príncipe.
- Two Gabonese fruiting collections (*Schoenmaker 13* and *van Valkenburg 2666*)
  resemble *T. alnifolia* in their long inflorescences, sepals appressed
  against the follicles and long-petiolate leaves, but differ by very dense
  stellate hairs on the lower leaf surface and long petiolar hairs (a character
  shared with *T. podotricha*). Their status awaits flowering material.
