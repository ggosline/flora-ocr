# *Diospyros abyssinica* "(Hiern) F. White"

**Family**: [[Ebenaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Diospyros]]

Shrub or small tree reaching 40 cm in diameter, with erect stem bearing a few small basal flanges; rhytidome very dark brown-purple, fissured both vertically and horizontally, thus forming small rectangular scales about 2 cm high and 1 cm wide; bark section with a dark brown outer band, then a dirty orange fibrous layer, then a bright orange layer adjacent to the white sapwood.

Leaves with petiole about 5 mm long; blade elliptic to lanceolate-elliptic, up to 10 x 3 cm, cuneate at the base and at the weakly acuminate apex; blade subcoriaceous, brown-blackish in dried material; 5-12 pairs of secondary veins fading toward the margin, that margin undulate in leaves of young shoots; tertiary lateral veins and veinlets dense and finely prominent, at least beneath; a few black glands scattered beneath the blade, often away from the midrib, especially around its middle portion.

According to the description and characters given by F. White, the single specimen collected in Cameroon is sterile but can nevertheless be assigned with great confidence to this species:

It may reach 75 cm in diameter and 35 m in height, though shrubs only 2 m tall may already flower and fruit. Heartwood white with a few black streaks, or entirely black in the center. Leaves up to 12 x 4 cm, with as many as 12 pairs of secondary veins. Male flowers 3-4-merous, in contracted cymes of 15-18 subsessile flowers in the leaf axils on young shoots or in the axils of leaf scars on 2-3-year-old twigs; bracteoles persistent, lanceolate, recurved at the tip, very shortly setulose; calyx reaching one third of the corolla, with acute triangular lobes, more or less ciliate on the margin, open in bud; floral bud swollen with obtuse tip; corolla cream-colored, 5-6 mm, deeply divided almost to the base into 3-4 broadly elliptic, obtuse, glabrous lobes; 10-16 glabrous stamens inserted at the base of the corolla in 2 ranks, with filaments 0.5-3 mm and apiculate anthers 1.5-3 mm long; pistillode rudimentary or absent. Female flowers 3-4-merous, usually in axillary cymules of 1-3-5(-8) flowers, on woody pedicels about 2 mm long, not accrescent; bracteoles pubescent, concave, caducous; flowering calyx glabrous, 2 mm long, divided almost to the base into strongly imbricate suborbicular lobes, sometimes apiculate, ciliate on the margin; cream corolla like that of the male flower; 0-4 filiform rudimentary staminodes; ovary conical, 4 x 2 mm, glabrous, 6-locular, with styles united into a short column slightly 3-lobed at the summit. Fruits ellipsoid-conical, yellow then red then black and glabrous at maturity, 1.4 x 0.9 cm, with persistent style remnant and only slightly accrescent calyx at the base; 1, rarely 2, seeds, globose to subellipsoid, 9 x 6 mm.

**Lectotype designated by F. White (1956):** Beccari 55, Eritrea (K; iso-FI).

This species has a very broad distribution, extending from Guinea to Ethiopia, then to Mozambique and Angola, skirting the whole Congo basin. It favors dry-forest formations and also occurs on mountain slopes, and is absent from the evergreen humid dense forests of western and central Africa. The same is true in Cameroon, where it has been found only on the edge of semi-deciduous humid dense forest north of Bertoua, on rocky hillsides, together with *Celtis zenkeri*, a typical species of the northern forms of that forest. This matches the ecological settings reported for specimens from Ghana, Ivory Coast, Nigeria, the Central African Republic, and the DRC.

It is worth noting that *D. abyssinica*, though morphologically close to *D. piscatoria* of evergreen humid dense forest, differs from it rather clearly in ecological terms.

### Material studied in Cameroon

Letouzey 2981，mont Sangembam entre Letta et Viali，4o km N Bertoua (ster.).

The sterile Paris specimen Letouzey 5070, collected 20 km south of Mboy I, 45 km east of Yokadouma, almost certainly also belongs here. It is a small tree called "Djama" in Bibaya Pygmy usage, reaching 40-50 cm in diameter, with hard pale-yellow wood containing a few black patches at the center; it is very abundant in the semi-deciduous humid dense forest with Sterculiaceae and Ulmaceae east and southeast of Yokadouma.
