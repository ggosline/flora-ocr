# *Triclisia dictyophylla* Diels

**Family**: [[Menispermaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Triclisia]]

In Engl., Pflanzenr. **IV.94**: 70 (1910).

## Synonymy

- *Triclisia gilletii* (De Wild.) Staner, Bull. Séances Inst. Roy. Colon.
  Belge **4**: 430 (1933).

## Description

Liana up to **at least 30 m** long; **stem, petiole and lower side of the
main leaf venation puberulent, glabrescent**. **Leaves**: petiole
cylindrical, **(3–)6–20 cm**; blade broadly ovate, **17–26 × 10–22 cm**,
obtuse to slightly cuneate or slightly cordate at base, acuminate over
1.5–2.5 cm at apex, coriaceous, **with 2 pairs of dominant basal nerves**
and 2 or 3 pairs of lateral nerves higher up.

**Inflorescence** in axillary panicles or on second-year branches,
tomentellous. **Male flower** subsessile; sepals in 3 or 4 cycles, the
smallest ovate, 0.5–1.5 mm long, the median 1.5–2.5 mm, the inner obovate,
**5–6.5 × 2.5–3.5 mm**; petals 6, ovate-elliptic, ± 1 × 0.5 mm, glabrous;
stamens 2.5–3.5 mm long. **Female flower** subsessile; petals
suborbicular, 0.6 × 0.6 mm, glabrous; carpels ± 15, with fine, dense
silvery pilosity.

**Fruit**: mericarp ellipsoidal, **2–2.5 × 2 × 1 cm, with a curved beak ±
5 mm long**, finely puberulent; mesocarp subfleshy, ± 2 mm thick when
dry; endocarp very hard. Seed 1–1.5 × 1 cm.

## Distribution

**Range**: Cameroon, Gabon, Republic of the Congo, Democratic Republic of
the Congo, Central African Republic, Angola.
**Gabon**: Moyen-Ogooué, Ogooué-Ivindo.

## Habitat and ecology

Degraded forest, riverine forest, secondary forest. In Gabon up to 300 m
altitude, elsewhere up to 960 m.

## Uses

In the Democratic Republic of the Congo, a decoction of branchlet bark is
commonly drunk to treat **fever and malaria**, as well as diarrhoea,
gastric disorders and purulent catarrh. In Republic of the Congo, root
juice is taken against rheumatism and joint inflammation; juice of young
leaves is given to mentally ill patients during crises as an analgesic.
In the Central African Republic, the root is used as an abortifacient and
emmenagogue. The root, especially the root bark, is an ingredient of
**arrow poisons**. In Gabon the plant is reputed febrifuge; a superstitious
custom uses the leaves to wash and dry small twins at birth.

## Vernacular names

murindi (ngowé); oréndé (nkomi).

## Notes

- Troupin (1962) had united *T. dictyophylla* and *T. gilletii*; Jongkind
  (2017) re-separated them. Breteler accepts a single species *T.
  dictyophylla* on morphological grounds (the type of *T. dictyophylla*,
  from Angola, in fact bears characters of "*T. gilletii*"). However the
  Gabonese material with **larger drupes and no beak**, previously
  treated as *T. dictyophylla* sensu Jongkind, is here described as a new
  species, [[Triclisia_megacarpa]].
