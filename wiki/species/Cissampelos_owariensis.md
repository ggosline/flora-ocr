# *Cissampelos owariensis* P.Beauv. ex DC.

**Family**: [[Menispermaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Cissampelos]]

Prodr. **1**: 100 (1824).

## Description

Slender **twining herb**; stem and petiole striate, at least when dry; stem,
leaf and inflorescence ± densely pubescent with stiff hairs, often with small
curved hairs beneath, sometimes purely tomentose on the lower leaf surface,
glabrescent or not. **Leaves**: petiole 2–10 cm, twisted and/or
curved-geniculate at base, with tomentum often denser at both ends; **blade
broadly ovate**, angular or not, **5–10(–13) cm long and wide**, generally
broader than long, rounded to subcordate at base, obtuse or acute and
mucronulate at apex, sometimes also at the angles, with **5–8 palmate basal
nerves**, prominent beneath.

**Male inflorescence** thyrsoid, up to 12 cm long (up to 40 cm outside Gabon),
with bracts ± early caducous, up to 6 mm wide; **female inflorescence**
racemose, up to 20(–33) cm long, with 5- to 8-flowered fascicles, with broad,
± persistent, **subreniform, membranous bracts** 1–1.5(–2) cm long.
**Male flower**: pedicel 0.5–1 mm; sepals spreading, oblong-obovate,
subentire to pinnately lobed, 1–1.5 × 0.7–1.2 mm, hairy outside; petals 0.2–2
mm; synandrium 0.2–1.2 mm. **Female flower**: pedicel 1.5–2 mm; sepal
oblong-ovate, 1.2–2 × 0.7–1 mm; petal truncate or subreniform, 1–1.2 mm long;
ovary 1–1.3 mm long, hairy; stigma 2- or 3-fid.

**Fruit** globose to subovoid, compressed or not, **4–6 × 4.5 mm**, hairy,
**pinkish-red at maturity**; endocarp 4 × 3 mm.

## Distribution

**Range**: Sierra Leone to Nigeria in West Africa, Cameroon to Democratic
Republic of the Congo and Angola, Central African Republic, Rwanda, Burundi
in central Africa, Tanzania and Zambia in eastern and southeastern Africa.
**Gabon**: Estuaire, Moyen-Ogooué, Ngounié, Ogooué-Maritime.

## Habitat and ecology

Bushland and secondary forest. In Gabon at 0–50 m altitude, elsewhere up to
900 m.

## Uses

Leaves and stems are taken in infusion against gastrointestinal complaints
(diarrhoea, dysentery, colic, intestinal worms, indigestion) and urogenital
problems (menstrual disorders, venereal diseases, infertility, childbirth).
Leaves and rhizomes are widely used to treat abscesses, ulcers and scabies,
and as a haemostatic. A decoction of stems mixed with leaves of other plants
is used to dress wounds. Stems serve as basketry-binding material. The plant
is occasionally cultivated as an ornamental.

## Vernacular names

liane amère, liane corde, liane serpent (fr.); bódji-bu-ili (bapunu);
boï-bu gili (bavarama, bavungu, éshira); dindji-toku-dinène (béséki); nguya
(bavili); ngwèndè a-pindi (mitsogo); nonyèmbè-nyèmbè-a-midjo (apindji);
owavi-ompolo (mpongwè); tsige (fang); tsikè (bakèlè).

## Notes

- The two collections cited by Sosef et al. (2006) under *C. mucronata*
  A.Rich. are here referred to *C. owariensis*.
- *C. pareira* L. has been reported for Gabon in the literature, but no
  herbarium voucher has been found to confirm its presence. It differs from
  *C. owariensis* by its male inflorescence reaching only 4 cm; the two
  species are often confused (see [[Cissampelos]] notes).
