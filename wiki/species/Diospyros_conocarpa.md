# *Diospyros conocarpa* "Gurke & K. Schum."

**Family**: [[Ebenaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Diospyros]]

Slender shrub or small tree, with straight stem reaching up to 10 m in height and 10 cm in diameter at the base, the branches arranged in pseudo-whorls toward the top of the bole. Bark dark green-black, marked with vertical cracks and fine papery scales that, when detached, reveal powdery purple patches; bark section with black outer rhytidome band and pale yellow fibrous inner layer; wood pale creamy yellow, hard, with blackish spots and veins toward the center. Young twigs green, with juvenile leaves amaranth pink. Older branchlets, often slightly zigzag, very dark purple, and, like the petioles, undersides of the veins, inflorescence axes, pedicels, and calyces, covered with a short pubescence mixed with scattered long, fairly stiff, yellowish-red hairs.

Leaves with petiole 5-8 mm long; blade oblong, often somewhat obovate, 18-25 x 5-9 cm including the acumen, with cordate base and rather acute acumen up to 25 x 10 mm; 6-8(-10) pairs of lateral veins, at least the lower ones subopposite, curved-ascending and loop-anastomosing near the margin; veins impressed on the glabrous upper surface, pubescent and hirsute beneath, at least in juvenile leaves; blade papery to subcoriaceous, glossy dark green above and pale green beneath when fresh, often reddish-brown in dried material; a few relatively large brown glands scattered beneath, especially along and slightly away from the midrib from the base into the acumen.

Among the normal leaves occur, here and there, small abnormal leaves, very shortly petiolate and scarcely exceeding 3 cm, folded along their curved midrib so that the upper surface becomes internal and glabrous, while the external lower surface remains pubescent and hirsute; this keeled transformed organ is swollen at the base and forms a cavity.

Field observation, something difficult to infer from ordinary herbarium specimens, shows that the young shoots are enclosed in pink scales surrounding the very young hirsute leaves, which are of a striking amaranth red. As the bud elongates, the proximal basal scales fall, leaving only the 2 distal scales at the base of the young shoot; these turn green and form the abnormal leaves in which ants nest. These abnormal scale-leaves persist for one or two growth periods at the base of the corresponding shoots.

This species is known from Nigeria, Cameroon, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, both Congos, and Cabinda, in evergreen humid dense forest, including the understory of *Gilbertiodendron dewevrei* stands.
