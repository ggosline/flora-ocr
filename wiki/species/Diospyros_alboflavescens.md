# *Diospyros alboflavescens* "(Gurke) F. White"

**Family**: [[Ebenaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Diospyros]]

## Full treatment

2.Diospyros alboflavescens (Gurke） F. White

Bull.Jard.Bot.Brux.26:24I (1956),p.p.(cf.D.iturensis (GURkE) R.LET. et F.WHITE).

- Maba alboflavescens GURkE,Bot.Jahrb.43 :199 (1909).

Specimens of this species appear in various herbaria under the following name: *D. alboflavescens* (Gurke ms.); cf. De Wildeman, *Pl. Bequaert.* 3:532 (1926).

$$ \mathrm {P L .} 2 0, I I - I 5, \mathrm {p .} 1 3 3, \text {e t} \mathrm {C A R T E} 2 8, \mathrm {p .} 1 6 9. $$

Tree reaching up to 20 m in height; branchlets glabrous, smooth, and black in dried material.

Leaves with petiole scarcely exceeding 1 cm, often deeply channelled; blade ovate-oblong to elliptic-oblong, 10-20 x 6-10 cm, rounded at the base, acute at the apex, usually with a broad triangular acumen ending in an obtuse tip; blade coriaceous, glabrous, with about 6 pairs of ascending arched lateral veins; veinlet network loose and faint; in dried material, blade very dark reddish-brown; a few crateriform glands occur near the midrib, especially near the base in the axils of the first lateral veins.

Male inflorescences in contracted cymes bearing up to 20 flowers, rarely axillary and normally produced on older branches and the trunk. Male flowers subsessile, with cup-shaped calyx 3 mm high, its rim truncate and sometimes torn so as to appear lobed, glabrous; corolla in bud an elongated tube, obtuse at the summit, yellowish, glabrous, 12 mm high at anthesis, with 3 thick lanceolate lobes reaching about three quarters of the corolla depth; 9 glabrous stamens inserted on the receptacle, with filaments scarcely exceeding 0.5 mm and elongate anthers about 2.5 mm long, obtuse at the tip; apparently no pistillode.

Female inflorescences, female flowers, and fruits unknown.

**Lectotype designated by F. White (1956):** Zenker 2273, Cameroon (P).

This species, represented only by 3 Zenker specimens from the Kribi region of Cameroon, is still very poorly known and clearly requires renewed field study, both to document the living tree and to obtain female flowers and fruits.
