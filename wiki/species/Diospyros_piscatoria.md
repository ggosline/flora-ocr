**Family**: [[Ebenaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Diospyros]]

PL.19,1-II,p.127,et CARTE 33,p. 174.

Tree reaching 30 m in height and 40-50 cm in diameter, though sometimes a shrub flowering from 5 m onward. Bole straight and slender, often fluted and sometimes with very small buttresses at the base; crown small, conical, and densely branched. Bark black, thin, exfoliating in fairly broad scales; outwardly, a 5 mm-thick black layer that powders under machete blows; inwardly, a yellow or orange layer, sometimes with a black band in the middle, 2-3 mm thick. Sapwood white or pinkish white; heartwood often black, but sometimes with greenish-brown veins. Branchlets slender, at first sparsely strigillose with tawny hairs, then glabrescent.

Leaves with petiole scarcely exceeding 5 mm; blade oblong-elliptic with nearly parallel margins for much of its length, up to 13 x 5 cm, with more or less obtuse base and caudate-acuminate apex, the acumen with nearly parallel margins and often retuse, sometimes slightly spatulate tip, reaching 2.5 x 0.3 cm; midrib narrow and deeply impressed above; 12-20 pairs of parallel secondary veins anastomosing 3-5 mm from the margin; blade papery, dark brown or dull black when dry; very young folded leaves covered with golden appressed bristles but soon becoming glabrous except for a few scattered hairs beneath the midrib and sparsely under the blade; a few glands occur on either side of and near the midrib in its lower part, sometimes higher, and almost always near the base of the acumen.

Male flowers, 3-4-merous, gathered 5-12 in rather loose axillary cymes about 1 cm long, with axes very shortly puberulent or setulose, borne on leafy twigs or on 2-3-year-old branchlets; pedicels slender, 2-3 mm, articulated at the summit, puberulent or setulose, bearing at the base a subpersistent ovate concave bracteole up to 2 mm long. Calyx with a lower part extending the pedicel above the articulation, glabrous, 1 mm long, and shield-shaped above, 2 mm high, glabrous but with a few small marginal cilia, with 1 mm tube and triangular lobes, rarely 5, each 1 mm high; corolla 5-6 mm high, acute or apiculate in bud, deeply lobed, often almost to the base, with broadly ovate-obtuse to obovate lobes, the margin near the summit at first recurved, then flat and entirely glabrous; 9-19 stamens, sometimes in pairs, attached at the base of the corolla tube, with filaments 0.5-2.5 mm, glabrous or puberulent, and narrowly subsagittate anthers about 2 mm long, apiculate, ciliate on the margins; pistillode ovoid, up to 4 x 1.5 mm, sometimes with rudimentary styles.

Female flowers, likewise 3-4-merous, solitary, more rarely 2-3 together, axillary or in the axils of leaf scars; pedicels 1-3 mm long, articulated at the summit, sparsely and shortly setulose, becoming woody in fruit and persisting after fruit fall, bearing persistent, broadly ovate, equally setulose bracteoles 1 mm long. Flowering calyx 6 mm high, slightly accrescent, glabrous except for slightly ciliate lobe margins, divided nearly to the base into broadly ovate lobes 4-5.5 x 3.5-4 mm, with obtuse and sometimes shortly apiculate apex; corolla a little longer than the calyx, reaching 7 mm, glabrous except for the slightly ciliate lobe margins, divided almost to the base into broadly ovate lobes 6 x 4 mm; 0-5 staminodes resembling the anthers and especially ciliate on the margin, but rudimentary and inserted at the base of the corolla tube; ovary ovoid, 3 mm, glabrous, 6-locular, and almost directly surmounted by 3 small superficially bilobed stigmas.

Fruits borne on woody persistent pedicels up to 8 mm long, subglobose, 1.2-1.5 cm in diameter, yellow then dull red then black when dry, with nearly smooth glossy surface, terminated at the top by the persistent style and surrounded at the base by the slightly accrescent calyx, then 8 mm long, whose lobes are often turned downward; seeds up to 6, measuring 12 x 5 x 3 mm.

**Lectotype designated by F. White (1956):** Zenker 3547, Cameroon (K; iso-BM, BR, E, MO, P, W).

Known from Guinea and Sierra Leone, this species is then distributed through the evergreen humid dense forests from Ghana to the mouth of the Congo; it often seems associated with disturbed or secondary forest. Morphologically close to *D. abyssinica*, it appears to differ rather clearly in its ecological requirements.

The bark and the fruit, including the calyx, are said to be ichthyotoxic.

Note: Close to *Diospyros piscatoria* is *Diospyros soubreana* F. White.

It is a shrub distributed from Ivory Coast to Nigeria in semi-deciduous humid dense forest only, with oblanceolate leaves narrowly triangular at the apex, fewer lateral veins, 7-10 pairs, male flowers with a less deeply lobed corolla, and almost sessile fruits with a somewhat larger fruiting calyx, 10-12 mm. Its presence in Cameroon, on the northern margins of humid dense forest, seems possible.
