# *Diospyros physocalycina* Gurke

**Family**: [[Ebenaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Diospyros]]

## Full treatment

25.Diospyros physocalycina Gürke

Bot.Jahrb.26:68 (1898).—M1LDBRAED,Notizbl.Bot.Gart.Berl.9 ：1052 (1926).- F.WHITE,FWTA ed.2,2: I1 (1963); Nigerian Trees 2: 335 (1964). -Heisteria Winkleri ENGL., Bot.Jahrb.43:169 (1909). - Diospyros xanthochlamys GURkE,Bot. Jahrb.43 : 2I0 (1909).

Specimens of this species appear in various herbaria under the following name: *Cyclostemon gabonense* (Pierre ms.), equal to *Drypetes gabonensis* and also misapplied to *Diospyros canaliculata*.

$$ \mathrm {P L .} 1 8, \mathrm {p .} 1 2 3, \mathrm {e t C a r t e} 3 4, \mathrm {p .} 1 7 5. $$

Shrub or small tree 10-15 m tall and 10-20 cm in diameter, with slender grey
twigs that are cracked in dried specimens and often, as in *D. canaliculata*,
bearing fine scattered corky pustules. Trunk bark black-greenish, with many fine
vertical wrinkles; in section black outwardly, cream-pink inside, quickly
turning dark brown, with a bright yellow cambial layer.

Leaves with a petiole channelled above, about 10 mm long; blade elliptic or
more often oblong-elliptic, up to 18(-25) × 7(-9) cm, acute or blunt at the
base and sometimes slightly decurrent onto the petiole, acuminate at the apex,
the acumen 10-20 × 3-5 mm and blunt-tipped. Midrib impressed above; 5-8 pairs
of lateral veins loop-anastomosing, with minor veins forming a very dense
network raised on both surfaces. Blade coriaceous, glabrous, the margin rolled
under after drying and then characteristically grey or grey-green; the lower
epidermis covered with more or less contiguous punctate mineral deposits. As in
*D. canaliculata*, which has a very similar leaf, black glands occur at the
junctions of minor veins, especially in the lower part of the blade, near both
the midrib and the margin, and often near the acumen.

Male flowers shortly but rather stoutly pedicellate, 1-3 mm, in fascicles of
1-5 or more in the leaf axils or on old twigs, with minute triangular bracts
and bracteoles ciliate on the margin. Calyx campanulate, 2-4 mm long, its
margin almost truncate with only 4 tiny teeth, clothed outside and sometimes
inside with a sparse short pubescence. The diagnosis of *D. xanthochlamys*
mentions a calyx seemingly lobed to the middle, but closer inspection of the
type shows that the apparent lobes are actually V-shaped tears bordered by scar
tissue; this anomaly recurs in several male-flowered specimens of
*D. physocalycina* and may cause confusion with *D. canaliculata*. Corolla
yellowish white, glabrous, with a narrow tube 8-10 × 4 mm, surmounted by 4
lanceolate lobes up to 7 mm long. Androecium of 16-18 stamens inserted at two
levels on the base of the corolla tube, often in pairs; filaments puberulent,
2-4 mm; anthers glabrous, 3-5 mm, apiculate. Pistillode apparently absent.

Female flowers solitary, on rather stout pedicels 1-5 mm long, with ciliate
bracts and bracteoles. Calyx accrescent, urceolate-cordiform, about 10 × 10 mm,
with 4 broad triangular lobes no more than 3 mm high, sparsely puberulent
outside and inside. Corolla with a broad tube about 6 × 5 mm, topped by 4
elliptic lobes about 6 × 4 mm, puberulent outside toward the base and middle.
Staminodes 0-8, small and lanceolate, attached at the base and mid-height of the
corolla tube. Ovary ovoid-conic, 2 mm high, glabrous except sometimes near the
style base, 4-locular, topped by 4 styles about 2 mm long, more or less fused
for 1 mm at the base and ending in capitate stigmas with a spreading papillose
surface.

Fruits on pedicels up to 5 mm long, ovoid, apiculate, about 2.5 cm high, red,
glabrous, almost completely hidden within the enlarged calyx, which reaches 3 cm
or more, is reddish yellow then dark red, urceolate-cordiform, widest just
above the base, with deltoid lobes much shorter than the lower part of the
calyx. Seeds up to 4, probably not exceeding 20 mm long.

**Type:** Zenker 915, Cameroon (iso-P).

This species is confined to Nigeria, Cameroon, and Gabon. Very close to
*D. canaliculata*, from which it differs best by the fruiting calyx, it seems to
prefer evergreen humid forest, whereas that species is more characteristic of
semi-deciduous forest. The existence of a fruiting specimen of
*D. physocalycina* from near Yokadouma in Cameroon, and perhaps another from
Central African Republic, may somewhat weaken that ecological distinction.
