# *Tacca leontopetaloides* (L.) Kuntze

**Family**: [[Taccaceae]] · **Genus**: [[Tacca]]

*Rev. gen. pl.* **2**: 704 (1891).

## Synonymy

- *T. pinnatifida* J.R.Forst. & G.Forst., *Char. gen. pl.*: 70 (1775)
- *T. involucrata* Schumach. & Thonn., *Beskr. Guin. Pl.*: 177 (1827)

## Description

Plant to **1.5(-2) m** high; rhizome globose to broadly ellipsoid, up to
**5 x 8 x 4 cm**.

**Cataphylls**: subsessile, linear-lanceolate, **8-21 x 1.2-3 cm**, rapidly
caducous.

**Leaves**: petiole **25-130(-180) x 1-3 cm**, hollow, pale green spotted
white-green to dark purple; blade to **90 x 60 cm**, palmatisect into 3 lobes
that are themselves pinnatifid, second-order lobes very variable in shape.

**Inflorescence**: scape **1(-2)**, **30-170 x 0.7-3 cm**, hollow, green;
involucral bracts **4-9(-12)**, ovate-lanceolate, up to **6 x 3 cm**, green,
outer bracts broader than the inner; flowers **20-40**; floral bracts
filiform, to **25 cm x 0.5 mm**, purple to blackish brown; pedicel under the
fruit to **7 cm x 2 mm**.

**Flower**: perianth tube **1.5-5 mm** long; outer lobes **4-7 x 3 mm**,
inner lobes **5-7 x 3-6 mm**. Stamens white to yellow, more rarely brown or
purple; filaments **3-6 x 2 mm**; thecae **2 x 2 mm**. Disk 3-lobed, with
glandular hairs. Ovary **3-5 x 2-4 mm**. Style **1.5-3 mm** long; stigmas
white to purple.

**Fruit**: globose to ovoid or ellipsoid, up to **3.5 x 2.5 cm**, changing
from green to orange.

**Seed**: **5-8 x 1.5-3 mm**, yellow-brown to red-brown.

## Distribution

**Range**: widely distributed through tropical Africa, Madagascar, tropical
Asia, and the Pacific as far as Polynesia.  
**Gabon**: widely distributed, especially in the coastal zone.

## Habitat and ecology

Common in savannas, open forests, roadsides, riverbanks and marshes, gallery
forest, beaches, and ruderal sites. In Gabon mainly at low altitude; elsewhere
to **1450 m**.

## Vernacular names

East Indian arrowroot, Polynesian arrowroot, Tahiti arrowroot (English);
Iboloboto (Mpongwe), bobolya, ampwal'intoto (Nkomi).

## Uses

The starch from the globose rhizomes is sometimes eaten, but only after long
soaking and prolonged boiling because the tubers are toxic when fresh. In Asia
the starch is also used medicinally against dysentery, diarrhoea, and oedema.
The fruit pulp is edible.

## Notes

The source emphasises that the species is both common and ecologically broad in
Gabon, but especially frequent toward the coast. It is also one of the clearest
examples in the wiki so far of a useful plant whose edible products require
careful detoxifying preparation.
