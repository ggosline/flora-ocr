# *Clematis* L.

**Authority**: L., Sp. Pl. **1**: 543 (1753), *nom. cons.*
**Family**: [[Ranunculaceae]]
**Synonym**: *Clematopsis* Bojer ex Hutch., Bull. Misc. Inform. Kew 1920: 12 (1920).

## Diagnosis

Lianas, or more rarely woody-based herbs, sometimes creeping, very rarely erect
(outside Gabon). Stems sarmentose, sometimes erect, with elongate internodes.
**Leaves opposite**, compound and ternate, imparipinnate, sometimes flabelliform,
rarely simple (outside Gabon); petioles, rachides and petiolules often twining;
leaflets entire, lobed or dentate at margin; principal nerves usually palmate;
tertiary nervation reticulate.

**Inflorescences** terminal or axillary, **trichotomously branched** (i.e.
ramifying in threes), in cymes, racemes or panicles of 3 to many flowers,
sometimes with solitary flowers. **Flowers** bisexual or sometimes unisexual,
actinomorphic, pedicellate; **sepals 4 or 5(–8), free, petaloid**, erect or
more often spreading at anthesis, with valvate-induplicate aestivation, caducous
in fruit; **petals absent**; nectaries present or absent; petaloid staminodes
rarely present; stamens numerous, inserted on a convex receptacle, with free
filaments often compressed, glabrous or partially villous, anthers 2-thecous;
carpels free, numerous, with 1 fertile ovule.

**Fruits**: numerous subsessile, rostrate achenes crowned by their styles,
which are usually accrescent and **plumose**.

A cosmopolitan genus of about **325 species**, comprising 9 subgenera and 18
sections, with greatest diversity in eastern Asia (nearly half the species
in China). In continental Africa, about 10–15 species (Brummitt 2000; Essig
2004; not counting the new species, varieties and subspecies of Wang 2000).

**In Gabon**: **two species**, both belonging to subgenus *Campanella* Tamura.
[[Clematis_grandiflora|*C. grandiflora*]] DC. belongs to section *Viorna*
(Pers.) Prantl, and [[Clematis_hirsuta|*C. hirsuta*]] Perr. & Guill. to
section *Brachiatae* Snoeijer (Wang 2000).

## Key to species

1. Inflorescence a **raceme**, composed of 1 terminal flower and 1–3 pairs of
   opposite flowers; **sepals greenish yellow**, 3–5 cm long, glabrous within,
   strongly **revolute at the apex**; leaflets ovate, with short and acute teeth;
   carpels up to **250**; style **7–10(–12) cm** in fruit
   → **[[Clematis_grandiflora|*C. grandiflora*]]**
1. Inflorescence in cymes united into a raceme or more often into a **panicle**,
   with 5 to many flowers; **sepals white**, 0.8–1.9(–2.4) cm long, ± densely
   puberulent within, acute or slightly apiculate, **not reflexed at apex**;
   leaflets coarsely lobed and dentate, sometimes entire; carpels **25–50**;
   style **2–4(–6) cm** in fruit
   → **[[Clematis_hirsuta|*C. hirsuta*]]** var. *hirsuta*

## Uses

Many species are valued as ornamentals; several dozen cultivars exist, often
with large showy flowers. The genus is also known for the toxic alkaloids it
produces (e.g. ranunculine, saponin), and a few species are used medicinally.

The treatment recognises both Gabonese species at species rank, follows Wang
(2000, 2004) for the subgeneric and sectional placement, and explicitly rejects

## Notes

- Both Gabonese *Clematis* species are **lianas with opposite, compound leaves
  and petaloid white-or-yellow sepals lacking true petals** — a morphological
  combination not at all typical of the family worldwide (most Ranunculaceae
  are herbs with alternate leaves and well-developed petals); see family
  diagnosis on [[Ranunculaceae]].
- Distribution of the two species suggests an ecological split:
  *C. grandiflora* is **hygrophilous, confined to wetter forests**; *C. hirsuta*
  prefers **drier savanna and even semi-arid regions**.
