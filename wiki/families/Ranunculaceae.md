# Ranunculaceae

**Authority**: Juss. (1789), *nom. cons.*
**Order**: Ranunculales (APG IV)
**Common name (FR)**: Renonculacées

## Diagnosis

Perennial or annual **herbs**, often with a ± developed rhizome, sometimes
suffrutex or **lianas**, without exudate. Stipules absent or rarely
rudimentary. **Leaves alternate, rarely opposite**, generally petiolate; petiole
often sheathing at base, sometimes transformed into a tendril; blade variously
compound, rarely simple, cordate, sometimes truncate to cuneate at base, with
margin entire or dentate; nervation pinnate or palmate, rarely parallel.

**Inflorescences** terminal or axillary, generally definite, rarely indefinite,
sometimes with solitary flowers; bracts or bract-like leaves sometimes present.
**Flowers** actinomorphic, sometimes zygomorphic, generally hermaphroditic,
sometimes unisexual, hypogynous, with **a single floral whorl of sepals**
(sometimes petaloid) **or with two whorls of sepals plus petaloid nectariferous
glands** (here called "petals"); bracteoles absent; sepals 3 to 6 or rarely
more, free, imbricate, sometimes valvate, caducous, sometimes persistent;
petals absent or 1 to 13, free, generally secreting nectar; **stamens many**,
free, with centripetal insertion, anthers with longitudinal dehiscence; gynoecium
of 1 or — more frequently — several free carpels, sometimes variously connate,
each with 1 or several anatropous (sometimes hemitropous) ovules.

**Fruits** syncarpous or apocarpous, sometimes solitary, in **follicles**,
more rarely **achenes**, berries or capsules. Seeds 1 to many per carpel;
endosperm abundant; embryo often undifferentiated at maturation; cotyledons
with petioles generally variously fused below, rarely solitary.

## Phylogenetic placement

A cosmopolitan, **monophyletic** family in the order **Ranunculales**, sister
to **Berberidaceae**; Berberidaceae + Ranunculaceae together are sister to
**[[Menispermaceae]]** (Stevens 2001; Wang et al. 2009).

Cossard et al. (2016) and Wang et al. (2009) recognise five subfamilies:
Glaucidioideae, Hydrastidoideae, Coptoideae, Thalictroideae and Ranunculoideae
(with 10 tribes). The last two are the largest in species number and the only
two with African representation; the first three are restricted to North
America and East Asia. The Gabonese genus [[Clematis]] belongs to subfamily
**Ranunculoideae, tribe Anemoneae**.

## Distribution

The family comprises about **62 genera and 2525 species** (Stevens 2001), the
majority herbaceous, exceptionally diversified in temperate and cold regions of
the northern hemisphere; greatest diversity in eastern Asia. In **Africa**,
six genera and **41 species** (Lebrun & Stork 2003), mostly in the mountainous
regions of East and southern Africa.

**In Gabon**: a single genus with two species — both lianas (highly atypical
for the family).

## Notes

- The general family description above (mostly **herbaceous**, with **alternate
  leaves**, often with **true petals**) **does not** apply to the Gabonese
  representatives. In Gabon the family is represented exclusively by **woody
  lianas with opposite, compound leaves, petaloid sepals, and no petals**.
  Readers used to the temperate-zone face of the family (buttercups,
  anemones, hellebores) should note this.
- Following Tamura (1993), this Flore uses "petals" as a practical term for
  any organ between the sepals and stamens (including what Prantl 1887 had
  called nectariferous leaves or staminodes). Strictly speaking, *Clematis*
  has none.
- The family is well known for **toxic alkaloids**; *Clematis* species
  contain ranunculine and saponin and are used in some medicinal traditions.
