### A new species of Diospyros (Ebenaceae) from Southwest
### Cameroon
GEORGE GOSLINE1 & MARTIN CHEEK'
Summary. Diospyros kupensis, a small treelet known only from Mt Kupe, Southwest Cameroon, is described
and illustrated. Its ecology, taxonomic affinities and unusual habit of vegetative reproduction are discussed.
INTRODUCTION
Letouzey & White (1970) describe 36 species of Diospyros from Cameroon (reduce
to 35 by the recognition of D. simulans F. White as a synonym of D. cinnabarin
### (Gfirke) F. White (White 1978)). All but six of these are found in evergre
### rainforest. Mt Kupe is the first high mountain (2100 m alt.) northeast of M
### Cameroon in the series of highlands and mountains that lie at the boundar
between West Africa and the Congo basin. Mt Kupe has been described as an isla
of rainforest "arising from a sea of farm-bush and banana plantations" (Collar &
Stuart 1988). The ecology and importance to conservation of the surviving fores
(all above 500 m alt.) on Mt Kupe, is summarized in Stoffelen et al. (1997) and in
Cheek & Cable (1996).
While we were conducting botanical inventory work on Mt Kupe in January -
February and October - November of 1995, few specimens of Diospyros were
encountered. D. obliquifolia (Hiern ex Girke) F. White, D. cinnabarina, and D.
pseudomespilus Mildbr. were encountered as isolated individuals. However, on the
south side of the mountain above Kupe village, one small, gregarious species of
Diospyros with conspicuous yellow-orange fruits was common. This has proven to be
a previously undescribed species, so far known only from the south side of Mt Kupe.

association with ants. The leaf bases are acute to obtuse, and the indumentum is
appressed subtomentose with greyish hairs less than 1 mm long. The male flowers
are in compressed glomerules with hirsute anthers. Flowering material of D.
kupensis has only very recently (April 1997, Etuge 3800) become available and
provides additional characters to distinguish the two species. In D. kupensis, the
ligulate sepals, 25 mm long, entirely conceal the much smaller corolla (c. 6 mm
long), which is only divided by about one third its length. However, in D.
conocarpa the sepals are ovate, only 20 mm long with the corolla about the same
size, but divided into lobes about two thirds its length.
When the leaves of D. kupensis are keyed through the vegetative key in Letouzey &
White (1970) the species arrived at is D. simulans (syn. D. cinnabarina), but D.
kupensis leaves lack the pale underside characteristic of D. cinnabarina.
D. kupensis is one of a number of small trees known so far only from Mt Kupe;
### Coffea montekupensis Stoffelen and Cola sp. nov. are other examples. All may well
occur in the Bakossi Mts to the west which are botanically poorly known.
A number of other species of Cameroonian Diospyros are also known from only
one or two restricted sites. D. platanoides and D. longiflora Letouzey & F. White are
each known from only two localities, and D. alboflavescens (Gfirke) F. White is known
from several collections at one locality.
D. kupensis has an unusual habit of vegetative reproduction. The tree is usually
monopodial, but the main "trunk" of some individuals was observed to lean at
about a 450 angle. Side shoots then adopt a vertical habit, and adventitious roots
develop beneath them. A group of 30 - 40 individuals apparently spreading in this
way has been observed by us at 1100 m on Mt Kupe.
